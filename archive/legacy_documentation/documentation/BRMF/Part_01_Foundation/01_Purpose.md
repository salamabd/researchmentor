## 1. Purpose

The Business Research Mentor (BRM) platform exists to develop competent, independent, reflective, and ethically responsible business researchers through structured AI-assisted mentoring that complements, rather than replaces, academic supervision.

BRM is an educational technology platform whose primary objective is to improve the quality of research thinking. Its purpose is not to generate dissertations, assignments, research articles, or other academic submissions on behalf of students, but to cultivate the intellectual capabilities required to produce high-quality scholarly work independently.

The platform is designed to function throughout the business research lifecycle, supporting students as they define research problems, evaluate evidence, identify gaps, formulate questions, justify theoretical and methodological choices, develop conceptual understanding, and make defensible research decisions.

At every stage:

- the student remains the author and owner of the research reasoning;
- the supervisor remains the academic authority;
- the AI acts as a structured educational mentor;
- the institution retains responsibility for academic governance;
- important recommendations and decisions must remain explainable and traceable.

---

## 2. Educational Mission

BRM exists to help students learn how researchers think.

Rather than emphasizing document production, the platform emphasizes research capability development through guided inquiry, structured reflection, evidence-based reasoning, constructive challenge, and iterative decision-making.

The platform therefore measures educational success primarily by the development of the student as a researcher, not by the number of pages written, the speed of document completion, or the volume of AI-generated text.

BRM should help students move from dependence on answers toward confidence in asking, examining, and resolving research questions independently.

---

## 3. Primary Educational Goal

The principal educational goal of BRM is to strengthen the student's ability to:

- identify meaningful and researchable business problems;
- distinguish broad interests from defensible research topics;
- formulate coherent research questions and objectives;
- critically evaluate academic and professional literature;
- identify assumptions, contradictions, limitations, and gaps;
- justify theoretical choices;
- identify, define, and evaluate constructs and variables;
- construct coherent conceptual frameworks;
- select and defend appropriate research designs;
- justify sampling, measurement, data collection, and analysis decisions;
- interpret supervisor feedback critically and constructively;
- revise research decisions when justified by evidence;
- recognize uncertainty and methodological limitations;
- develop confidence and independence as a researcher;
- act in accordance with academic integrity and research ethics.

---

## 4. Platform Purposes

BRM has five complementary purposes.

### 4.1 Student Development

Develop research competence, scholarly independence, and reflective judgement rather than merely facilitating document production.

### 4.2 Supervisor Support

Enhance supervisory effectiveness by providing structured insight into student reasoning, progress, unresolved questions, and research decisions without diminishing the supervisor's academic authority.

### 4.3 Program Quality

Provide program directors with ethical and proportionate indicators that support quality assurance, workload planning, milestone monitoring, early intervention, and student success while respecting privacy and institutional governance.

### 4.4 Institutional Improvement

Support business schools in strengthening research education through consistent mentoring processes, transparent milestones, evidence-informed program evaluation, and clearer visibility of student development.

### 4.5 Educational Research

Create a platform capable of supporting future scholarship into research mentoring, AI-assisted learning, doctoral education, research capability development, and supervision quality through governed and explainable educational data.

---

## 5. What BRM Is

BRM is:

- an AI-assisted business research mentoring platform;
- a research-thinking environment;
- a research decision support environment;
- a structured learning system for research capability development;
- a collaborative workspace connecting students and supervisors;
- a quality-support environment for program directors;
- a knowledge-driven educational platform;
- an explainable mentoring system;
- a platform for documenting how research reasoning develops over time.

---

## 6. What BRM Is Not

BRM is not:

- a dissertation generator;
- an assignment generator;
- a thesis-writing service;
- a literature review generator;
- a publication generator;
- a plagiarism avoidance tool;
- a system for disguising AI-generated writing;
- an AI substitute for a supervisor;
- an automated academic assessor;
- a system that makes unreviewable research decisions;
- a platform that obscures the origin of scholarly ideas or text;
- a surveillance system for unrestricted monitoring of students or supervisors.

## 7. Foundational Concept One: Research Decision Intelligence

### 7.1 Definition

Research Decision Intelligence (RDI) is the structured capability of the platform to support, document, connect, explain, and review significant research decisions throughout the research lifecycle.

BRM treats the research decision—not the generated paragraph—as a fundamental educational unit.

A research project develops through a sequence of connected decisions. These may include:

- selecting and refining a research topic;
- defining the research problem;
- determining the unit of analysis;
- identifying a population or context;
- formulating research questions;
- selecting or rejecting variables;
- choosing a theoretical perspective;
- defining relationships among constructs;
- selecting a research design;
- choosing a sampling approach;
- selecting measurement scales;
- determining data collection methods;
- selecting analytical techniques;
- interpreting limitations;
- revising decisions following evidence or feedback.

### 7.2 Decision Record

Each significant research decision should be capable of recording:

- the issue under consideration;
- the stage of the research lifecycle;
- the alternatives examined;
- the evidence or literature consulted;
- assumptions made;
- uncertainties identified;
- AI questions, prompts, and recommendations;
- the student's reasoning;
- the student's selected decision;
- the student's justification;
- supervisor comments or instructions;
- approval, revision, rejection, or pending status;
- links to preceding and dependent decisions;
- a complete revision history.

### 7.3 Educational Purpose

RDI is intended to make reasoning visible.

It enables the platform to help students understand that research quality depends not only on the final output but on the defensibility, coherence, and traceability of the decisions that produced it.

RDI should help students:

- compare alternatives instead of accepting the first plausible option;
- explain why a decision was made;
- recognize how one decision constrains later decisions;
- revisit earlier reasoning when new evidence emerges;
- prepare more effectively for supervisor meetings;
- demonstrate intellectual development over time;
- distinguish student judgement from AI recommendation.

### 7.4 Governance Principle

AI may inform, challenge, or structure a research decision, but it must not silently make the decision on behalf of the student.

The student remains responsible for the decision. The supervisor remains responsible for academic judgement and approval where required.

### 7.5 Research Decision Graph

Over time, connected research decision records may form a Research Decision Graph showing:

- dependencies among decisions;
- unresolved contradictions;
- changes in reasoning;
- evidence supporting decisions;
- supervisor interventions;
- the evolution of the research design.

The Research Decision Graph is a future platform capability and does not authorize premature architectural implementation before the educational framework is approved.

## 8. Foundational Concept Two: Research Capability Model

### 8.1 Definition

The Research Capability Model (RCM) is the structured educational model used to describe, develop, and assess the student's growth as a business researcher.

BRM must not equate research progress solely with chapter completion, word count, document submission, or elapsed time.

A student may complete a chapter without demonstrating sufficient research understanding. Conversely, a student may make substantial intellectual progress before producing a polished document.

The RCM therefore focuses on capability development.

### 8.2 Initial Capability Domains

The initial Research Capability Model should include:

1. **Problem Framing**  
   Ability to identify, delimit, and justify a meaningful business research problem.

2. **Question Formulation**  
   Ability to develop researchable, coherent, and aligned research questions.

3. **Literature Engagement**  
   Ability to search, select, compare, evaluate, synthesize, and critique relevant literature.

4. **Gap Reasoning**  
   Ability to distinguish a genuine research opportunity from a superficial absence of studies.

5. **Theoretical Reasoning**  
   Ability to select, compare, apply, and justify theoretical perspectives.

6. **Construct and Variable Reasoning**  
   Ability to identify, define, distinguish, operationalize, and relate constructs and variables.

7. **Conceptual Modelling**  
   Ability to develop coherent conceptual relationships grounded in theory and evidence.

8. **Methodological Judgement**  
   Ability to choose and justify research philosophy, approach, design, methods, and procedures.

9. **Evidence-Based Decision-Making**  
   Ability to support research decisions with appropriate evidence and transparent reasoning.

10. **Critical Reflection**  
    Ability to examine assumptions, recognize limitations, respond to feedback, and revise judgements.

11. **Research Integrity**  
    Ability to demonstrate authorship, transparency, ethical conduct, proper attribution, and responsible use of AI.

12. **Scholarly Independence**  
    Ability to make increasingly autonomous and defensible research decisions appropriate to the student's qualification level.

### 8.3 Developmental Principle

Research capability should be interpreted developmentally.

Expected performance will differ among:

- Honours students;
- MBA students;
- MSc Business students;
- DBA candidates;
- PhD candidates.

The platform should not apply identical expectations to all learners. Mentoring depth, challenge, independence, evidence requirements, and supervisor escalation should be appropriate to the student's program and stage.

### 8.4 Evidence of Capability

Capability should not be inferred solely from self-report or AI interaction frequency.

Evidence may include:

- quality of student justifications;
- ability to compare alternatives;
- alignment across research decisions;
- use and evaluation of evidence;
- response to supervisor feedback;
- recognition of limitations;
- correction of contradictions;
- increasing independence;
- improvement across repeated decision cycles.

### 8.5 Use of Capability Indicators

Capability indicators are educational aids, not automated grades.

They may support:

- student reflection;
- supervisor preparation;
- formative feedback;
- identification of support needs;
- milestone readiness discussions;
- program-level improvement.

They must not replace formal academic assessment or supervisor judgement unless a future institution explicitly adopts a validated assessment model under separate governance.

## 9. Relationship Between RDI and RCM

Research Decision Intelligence and the Research Capability Model are complementary.

RDI records and connects the student's research decisions.

RCM interprets the educational capabilities demonstrated through those decisions.

Together, they allow BRM to ask:

- What decision is the student making?
- Why is the student making it?
- What evidence supports it?
- What alternatives were considered?
- What capability does the decision demonstrate?
- Where is further development required?
- When should the supervisor intervene?

This relationship establishes the foundation for a platform centered on researcher development rather than text generation.

## 10. Intended Educational Outcomes

Students using BRM should progressively demonstrate:

- improved critical thinking;
- stronger academic reasoning;
- deeper engagement with literature;
- more defensible research decisions;
- greater ability to compare alternatives;
- stronger alignment among problems, questions, objectives, theory, and method;
- improved justification of methodological choices;
- increased reflective capacity;
- greater scholarly independence;
- improved preparation for supervision;
- greater awareness of academic integrity and research ethics.

These outcomes define the educational success of the platform.

---

## 11. Core Success Criterion

Every proposed feature shall be evaluated against the following question:

> Does this feature measurably improve the student's ability to become a better business researcher?

If the answer is no, the feature shall not be included in BRM Version 1.

If the answer is yes, the feature must also demonstrate that it:

1. promotes independent learning;
2. develops one or more defined research capabilities;
3. supports or improves research decision quality;
4. preserves academic integrity;
5. strengthens rather than weakens the student–supervisor relationship;
6. provides explainable educational value;
7. respects privacy and institutional governance;
8. aligns with BRMF principles;
9. does not encourage academic dependency on AI;
10. can be evaluated through observable educational outcomes.

---

## 12. Guiding Principle

BRM is founded on the belief that excellent researchers are developed through disciplined thinking, reflective practice, informed decision-making, constructive challenge, and effective mentorship.

Artificial intelligence should accelerate learning, not replace it.

The ultimate measure of BRM's success is not the quantity or fluency of text produced through the platform, but the quality, independence, integrity, and judgement of the researchers who emerge from using it.

---

## 13. Educational Implications

This chapter requires BRM to:

- prioritize reasoning workflows over writing workflows;
- ask students to justify significant decisions;
- preserve evidence of intellectual development;
- differentiate mentoring by qualification level;
- identify capability growth and support needs;
- include supervisor review at appropriate decision points;
- prohibit features whose primary purpose is academic text production;
- maintain clear boundaries between suggestion, decision, and approval.

---

## 14. Platform Implications

Future platform design should support:

- structured research decision records;
- links among related research decisions;
- student-authored justifications;
- AI-generated questions and explainable recommendations;
- supervisor review and feedback;
- decision status and revision history;
- capability-aligned mentoring activities;
- formative capability indicators;
- privacy-aware program analytics;
- traceability from features to educational principles.

These implications are directional requirements only. They do not authorize implementation before BRMF and the remaining foundation documents are reviewed and accepted.
